ESAS PCOC essentials

From distress to severity. SAS asked: How much distress is your X causing you? ESAS PCOC asks: How problematic is X for you? The score is severity as problem, not distress.

Patient rated severity over the last 24 hours, 0–10. Replaces sas. Eleven symptoms plus an Other slot. Order is fixed: physical first, psychological next, wellbeing last as a global question.

  • Pain
  • Tiredness
  • Drowsiness
  • Nausea
  • Appetite
  • Shortness of breath
  • Constipation
  • Sleep
  • Depression
  • Anxiety
  • Wellbeing
  • Other (D dry/sore mouth · I itch · S swallowing · Ot other · N none)

* reverse scored.   Bold: new in V4 (not in SAS).   Enter N/A if any item is not assessed.

Reverse scored items (appetite, sleep, wellbeing): 0 means best possible, not absent. Terminal-phase patient with no appetite who is not troubled by it scores 0/10. Elicit ratings in conversation, not by asking for a number on demand.

The patient facing scale

What we hand to the patient: six colour zones, four descriptors, six smiley anchors. The ESAS asks: What best describes how you felt during the last 24 hours? A family member, carer or staff member can help if needed.

Rated by, and response thresholds

Rated by records who scored: P patient (preferred) · F family or carer · C clinician (only if no family or carer is available) · O other. A proxy rates from the patient's perspective, not their own.

0 123 45 67 89 10
0–3 Anticipate & monitor. Modify the plan as required.
4–7 Review, intervene, refer.
8–10 Urgent. Same shift response.

Delirium presence on the PCOC form

The PCOC form now asks one delirium question on every assessment: Is delirium present? Yes / No / Not Assessed. Clinician rated, on observation of the patient's attention, awareness and cognition. Definition (Manual p.20): an acute and fluctuating disturbance in attention, awareness and cognition that often reflects an underlying medical, psychological or pharmacological cause.

Why it matters. Delirium is potentially reversible with timely management. Without structured screening, early detection is poor and many cases are missed, particularly the hypoactive presentation, which is easy to overlook. Recording delirium in PCOC V4 is the prompt to act on it [PINCHES ME, see further information].

4AT: quick reference

When to use. The 4AT is a screening tool, not a monitoring tool. It is not necessary to repeat the 4AT on every assessment once delirium is established. Use it when you are uncertain whether delirium is present. Exception: if terminal delirium or terminal agitation has been confirmed by a senior clinician, the 4AT is not required.

Self check: four scenarios

#Scenario & questionAnswer
i Caoimhe, 72, motor neurone disease, seen weekly at home. ESAS today: pain 1, tiredness 4, sleep 3, others 2 or below. She self rates. What does sleep 3 mean? What phase, what response? Sleep 3 is mildly poor. Sleep is reverse scored: 0 means best possible. Stable phase, no plan of care change. Anticipate and monitor. Tiredness 4 sits in the moderate band on its own and prompts a symptom review. Rated by: P.
ii Brendan, 79, advanced heart failure, day 3 in the unit. Pleasant, chatty. ESAS: pain 2, tiredness 3, sleep 2, others 2 or below. Asked about wellbeing he says “a 7, I'm doing alright.” Do you record 7? No. Wellbeing is a global question (how the patient feels overall) and is reverse scored: 0 = best wellbeing, 10 = worst. He feels well, so the score is low: 2 or 3. Recheck with the patient scale; the smiling face is on the left at 0.
iii Aoife, 84, advanced ovarian cancer, day 4 in unit, intermittently agitated. Daughter at the bedside. Is delirium present? Do you 4AT? Who rates the ESAS? Delirium present: Yes, on clinician judgement. 4AT not needed once delirium is clinically clear. Daughter rates the ESAS from her mother's perspective. Rated by: F. Phase already terminal. Adjust the plan to manage agitation in line with goals of care.
iv Seamus, 68, advanced pancreatic cancer, “a bit vague” per the team. You are unsure whether he is delirious. 4AT: alertness 0, AMT4 1, attention 2, acute change 4. What is the score? What next? Total 7. Possible delirium. Document delirium present: Yes on the PCOC form. Communicate to MDT. Identify and treat reversible causes in line with goals of care (medication review, hydration, sleep, environment, sensory aids). Reassess clinically. Repeating the 4AT is not necessary once delirium is established.
